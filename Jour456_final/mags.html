Notable Fashion Magazines During
the mid-late 1800s

Godey's Lady's Book, Harper's Bazaar and Ringwood's Afro-American Journal of Fashion

Godey's Lady's Book (1837-1896)

Godey’s Lady’s Book is regarded as the first magazine to really focus on the topic of fashion. However, this magazine was not as fashion oriented as some later ones because of editing differences between the editor, Sarah Josepha Hale, and the owner of the magazine, Louis Godey [1].

Godey’s started as a merger between two publications. The first of these publications was Ladies’ Magazine, which was edited by Hale [2]. However in 1837, Louis Godey bought Hale’s magazine and merged it with his own Lady’s Book. When Godey bought the magazine, he brought on Hale as the editor and thus began her career for Godey’s Lady’s Book [3].

Despite her role at the magazine and her contribution towards its future successes, Godey only would give her the title of “Literary editor” even though she was second only to him in the hierarchy of the magazine [4].

Before Hale came to the magazine, Lady’s Book rarely published original content because there were not copy-right laws at the time. This practice changed once she began as editor, allowing the magazine to reach new heights in popularity and become the most popular women’s magazine in the mid-19th century [5].

Under Hale’s leadership, Godey’s Lady’s Book became an 84-page monthly magazine by the 1850s and reached a circulation of 150,000 right before the beginning of the Civil War [6].

Like many magazines at the time, Godey’s Lady’s Book was geared towards upper-class women [7]. It mostly published literature and poetry in its beginnings and Hale set strict guidelines for what content could be included.

Hale wanted the literature in the magazine to be moral and pure, which eventually led to the publication's downfall during the Civil War because she did not believe talk about an ongoing war met these criteria [8].

Besides literature and poetry, Godey’s Lady’s Book included topics like recipes, handicrafts, patterns, beauty, gardening, interior decoration, musical scores, fashion plates and fashion chit-chat [9]. However, the fashion plates found dispersed in the pages of the magazine were by far the most popular feature.

These fashion plates would typically feature the latest styles from Paris and offered a diverse selection of models for women to imitate. Many women would even rip these plates out of the magazine to use as home-decor [10].

The fashion plates included in the magazine were typically created by wood carvings and then hand colored in Philadelphia at the magazine's plant or by women artists working from home. However, the women who would color these fashion plates were typically unsupervised, which led to discrepancies in the colors of the dresses. Some readers would be confused when they would compare their plates to their friends who had different colors included [11].

Even though the fashion plates helped the magazine gain traction, Hale adamantly despised the inclusion of them. She considered the fashions to be frivolous and preferred that domestic talent be promoted in order to help display a more American sense of style [12].

During the Civil War, subscription for Godey’s steeply dropped and the publication was out run by cheaper publications that were released in the latter part of the century.

Harper's Bazaar (1867-present)

Harper’s Bazaar, which originally was spelled “Bazar” until 1929, is regarded as the first fashion magazine in the sense that it is purely devoted to fashion [13]. Rather than being targeted towards the mass market, like Godey’s Lady’s book was, it was specifically tailored to those who cared about fashion [14].

When Harper’s Bazaar was first released in 1867, the market for women’s magazines was still largely untapped despite women making up the majority of magazine subscribers. Women wanted more options for magazines that were tailored to them and Harper’s Bazaar delivered [15].

Harper’s Bazaar was founded by Harper & Brother, and they originally brought on Mary Booth to be their editor until she passed away in 1889 [16]. In the first edition, the magazine described itself as “A Repository of Fashion, Pleasure, and Instruction” [17].

In the first 10 years after publication, the magazine reached an impressive circulation of 80,000 and only continued to grow in popularity afterwards. For decades after its original release, Harper’s Bazaar was considered the biggest and most successful fashion magazine [18][19].

The magazine was originally inspired by the German magazine, Der Bazar, which is where Fletcher Harper also got the name for the magazine from.

Aimed at upper-middle class women, the magazine expressed preferences towards more luxurious things and fashions [20]. The pictures of the latest fashions typically came from etchings delivered from Der Bazar to New York, where Harper’s Bazaar was published. These fashion plates usually were large and would sometimes even span across multiple pages in the magazine [21].

Mary Booth would also include etchings and carvings by well known artists and cartoonists like Winslow Homer and Thomas Nast [22].

At this point, the majority of the fashions displayed were still coming from Paris and Berlin, however they were sometimes modified to better fit the U.S. market. As the magazine gained more traction, Booth also had her staff make reports on the latest fashions in New York [23].

While the majority of the magazine was dedicated to fashion, Booth also included things like short stories, poems, articles about manners, health, furniture and household management. There were also sections dedicated to gossip and humor [24].

Even today, Harper’s Bazaar remains one of the most popular fashion magazines in the market and its early entrance into the market was extremely important towards its future successes.

Ringwood's Afro-American Journal of Fashion (1891-1895)

Ringwood's Afro-American Journal of Fashion was a fashion magazine geared towards African-American women in the late 1800s. Not only was it the first fashion magazine for African-American women, but it was also the first popular magazine aimed towards this group [25].

The magazine ranged from 12-16 pages long and showcased some of the latest Parisian fashions and sketches of prominent Black women. It also included things like dressmaking patterns, articles about health, poetry and love stories. In its prime, the magazine reached a circulation of about 4,000 copies per month [26].

Ringwood’s was founded by Julia Ringwood Coston in 1891 in Cleveland, Ohio and was distributed throughout the United States and the Caribbean [27]. She saw a gap in what white magazines were covering and created the Ringwood's so that the interests of Black women could be represented in a magazine [28].

The magazine would display pictures, articles, obituaries, and biographical sketches of African-American women who Coston found to be outstanding members of the community. She thought that it was important to represent these elite women as modelhoods of ladyhood and to show that despite society's assumptions, African-American women could be and are ladylike [29].

Coston found fashion to be the perfect way to help combat the negative assumptions about Black women during this period and thought it could also help uplift the whole community regardless of class.

However, when showing images of fashion and fashionable women, the magazine typically portrayed white women [30].

Still, the magazine offered a forum where race was connected to fashion and allowed working class African-American women to see role models from their community.

While much of the information about Ringwood’s was lost to history, the importance that the magazine had towards Black women during this period is not. Ringwood’s had supporters across the country and readers enjoyed both the writings and drawings included within it [31].
